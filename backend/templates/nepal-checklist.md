# Nepal Trip Planning Checklist

Your comprehensive guide to planning a safe and unforgettable Nepal adventure.

---

## Before You Go

- [ ] Apply for Nepal visa online (Tourist On Arrival or e-Visa at welcomenepal.com)
- [ ] Get travel insurance that explicitly covers high-altitude trekking and helicopter evacuation
- [ ] Book your Trekkers' Information Management System (TIMS) card in advance
- [ ] Arrange Annapurna Conservation Area Permit (ACAP) or Everest / Langtang permits as required
- [ ] Check vaccination requirements: Hepatitis A & B, Typhoid, Rabies, and routine shots
- [ ] Book flights to Tribhuvan International Airport (KTM) and domestic flights (Lukla, Pokhara) early
- [ ] Register your trip itinerary with your country's embassy in Kathmandu
- [ ] Research your specific trek route — teahouse vs. camping, guide requirements, difficulty

---

## Packing Essentials

### Clothing Layers
- [ ] Moisture-wicking base layers (merino wool or synthetic — no cotton)
- [ ] Insulating mid-layer: fleece jacket or down vest
- [ ] Warm down jacket for evenings and high altitude (4,000 m+)
- [ ] Waterproof outer shell / rain poncho
- [ ] Trekking trousers (zip-off style is versatile)
- [ ] Warm hat, balaclava, and gloves

### Trekking Gear
- [ ] Sturdy waterproof trekking boots (broken in at home before the trip)
- [ ] Lightweight camp shoes or sandals for evenings
- [ ] Trekking poles — essential for steep descents and river crossings
- [ ] Sleeping bag rated to -10°C for high-altitude teahouses
- [ ] Daypack (25–35 L) and a larger duffel for porters
- [ ] Headlamp with spare batteries or a USB-rechargeable model

### Medical Kit
- [ ] Blister pads (Compeed or equivalent)
- [ ] Diamox (acetazolamide) for altitude sickness prevention — consult your doctor
- [ ] Ibuprofen and paracetamol
- [ ] Oral rehydration salts (ORS sachets)
- [ ] Antiseptic wipes and wound dressings
- [ ] Antidiarrhoeal medication (Imodium)
- [ ] Antihistamines and any personal prescription medication

## Money & Connectivity

- [ ] Withdraw Nepalese Rupees (NPR) at the Kathmandu airport ATM on arrival
- [ ] Carry USD 100–200 in cash as backup (widely accepted at tourist spots)
- [ ] Get a Ncell or NTC prepaid SIM card at the airport (bring a passport copy)
- [ ] Note: card payments are limited; ATMs are rare above 3,000 m — carry enough cash
- [ ] Budget NPR 2,000–5,000 per day for teahouse trekking (food, bed, snacks)
- [ ] Download offline maps before entering the mountains: Maps.me or Google Maps offline
- [ ] Install a VPN if you need consistent access to blocked services

## Health & Safety

- [ ] Learn to recognise Acute Mountain Sickness (AMS) symptoms: headache, nausea, dizziness
- [ ] Follow the golden rule: "climb high, sleep low" — never ascend more than 500 m/day above 3,000 m
- [ ] Drink 3–4 litres of water daily at altitude to prevent dehydration
- [ ] Always use water purification: iodine tablets, SteriPen, or a filter bottle
- [ ] Save emergency contacts before you go:
  - CIWEC Clinic Kathmandu: +977 1 4435232
  - HAMS Hospital Kathmandu: +977 1 4444800
  - Tourist Police: 1144
  - Nepal Emergency: 100
- [ ] Identify helicopter rescue evacuation points on your trek route
- [ ] Never trek solo in remote or high-altitude areas — hire a licensed guide or join a group
- [ ] Purchase trip cancellation insurance in case of flight delays (Lukla flights cancel frequently)

## Cultural Etiquette

- [ ] Remove shoes before entering temples, monasteries, and local homes
- [ ] Dress modestly at religious sites — cover shoulders and knees
- [ ] Walk clockwise around stupas, chortens, and mani walls
- [ ] Ask explicit permission before photographing people, rituals, or ceremonies
- [ ] Offer donations at temples in cash rather than sweets or pens to children
- [ ] Tip your guide (USD 10–15 per day) and porter (USD 7–10 per day)
- [ ] Use your right hand or both hands when giving or receiving items
- [ ] Avoid pointing the soles of your feet at people or sacred objects
- [ ] Do not touch religious statues or sacred items unless invited to do so

## Top Things to Do

### Kathmandu Valley
- [ ] Pashupatinath Temple — sacred Hindu cremation ghats on the Bagmati River
- [ ] Boudhanath Stupa — the world's largest stupa and a UNESCO World Heritage Site
- [ ] Swayambhunath (Monkey Temple) — panoramic views of Kathmandu Valley
- [ ] Patan Durbar Square — medieval palaces and intricate Newari architecture
- [ ] Bhaktapur Durbar Square — the best-preserved medieval city in Nepal

### Trekking
- [ ] Annapurna Base Camp (ABC) trek — 7–12 days, moderate difficulty
- [ ] Annapurna Circuit — 14–21 days, includes Thorong La Pass (5,416 m)
- [ ] Everest Base Camp (EBC) trek — 12–16 days, via Lukla
- [ ] Langtang Valley trek — 7–10 days, close to Kathmandu, less crowded

### Wildlife & Nature
- [ ] Chitwan National Park — jungle safari, one-horned rhinos, Bengal tigers
- [ ] Bardia National Park — wilder and less crowded than Chitwan
- [ ] Phewa Lake, Pokhara — kayaking and the famous Fishtail (Machhapuchhre) reflection

### Spiritual & Cultural
- [ ] Lumbini — birthplace of Siddhartha Gautama (Buddha), UNESCO World Heritage Site
- [ ] Muktinath Temple — sacred to both Hindus and Buddhists in the Mustang region

### Viewpoints
- [ ] Nagarkot sunrise — views of the Himalayan range including Everest
- [ ] Sarangkot sunrise near Pokhara — Annapurna and Dhaulagiri at dawn
- [ ] Paragliding in Pokhara — tandem flights over Phewa Lake

## Best Time to Visit

| Season | Months | Conditions |
|--------|--------|------------|
| **Peak (Autumn)** | Oct – Nov | Clear skies, ideal for trekking and mountain photography |
| **Spring** | Mar – May | Rhododendrons in bloom, warmer, good trekking conditions |
| **Winter** | Dec – Feb | Cold at altitude, quiet trails, cheaper accommodation |
| **Monsoon** | Jun – Sep | Lush landscapes, leeches on trails, avoid high passes |

- [ ] Book flights and popular lodges 3–6 months in advance for the Oct–Nov season
- [ ] Spring is excellent for lower elevations and flower photography
- [ ] Winter is ideal for budget travellers who want fewer crowds
- [ ] Some treks (e.g. Upper Mustang, Dolpo) are best done during monsoon as they are rain-shadow areas
