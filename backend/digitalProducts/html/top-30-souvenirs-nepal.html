Shopping in Nepal: The Essentials

Souvenirs 1–10: Art, textiles, and iconic Nepali objects

Nepal produces some of the finest handicrafts in Asia — from intricate Thangka paintings requiring months of skilled work, to hand-hammered singing bowls tuned to precise frequencies, to luxurious Pashmina shawls of unmatched softness. The key to shopping well in Nepal is knowing what authentic looks like, understanding fair price ranges, and choosing shops that support traditional craftspeople rather than mass-produced tourist tat. Thamel is convenient but often overpriced; the artisan quarters of Patan, Bhaktapur, and the craft villages of the Valley consistently offer better quality at fairer prices. Always bargain — the first price quoted is almost never the final price — and allow time, because the best pieces take time to find.

1
Thangka Painting
NPR 3,000–500,000+Boudhanath, Patan, Thamel

Thangka are sacred Buddhist scroll paintings on cotton or silk, depicting deities, mandalas, and cosmological diagrams in mineral pigments and gold. A quality thangka is painted by a trained artist following strict iconographic conventions, with faces and hands receiving the most careful attention. Sizes range from palm-sized to room-filling; subjects include Medicine Buddha, Green Tara, Chenrezig, Wheel of Life, and the 84 Mahasiddhas. The finest come from Boudhanath's thangka painting schools.

Authenticity tip: A genuine hand-painted thangka takes weeks to months to complete. If it costs less than NPR 3,000 it is almost certainly a printed reproduction. Examine under magnification — brush strokes should be visible, especially in fine details. Ask to see the artist at work in the studio.

2
Pashmina Shawl
NPR 2,500–25,000Thamel, Durbar Marg, Patan

True Pashmina is the ultra-fine undercoat fibre of the Changthangi goat, hand-combed from the animal's belly in spring. The finest quality (pure Pashmina, 100%) is extraordinarily soft, warm, and lightweight. Most shawls sold as "Pashmina" in Nepal are blends — typically 70% Pashmina / 30% silk (which adds lustre and strength) or lower-quality mixes with viscose or acrylic. Pure Pashmina is hand-woven on a loom and has slight irregularities; machine-woven fabric is perfectly uniform.

Authenticity tip: The burn test: pure Pashmina burns like hair (smells like burning hair, produces crushable ash). Synthetic fibres melt or produce plastic smell. The ring test: a genuine Pashmina shawl passes through a finger ring. Certificates of authenticity from reputable shops are available but easily forged — buy from established vendors.

3
Singing Bowl
NPR 1,500–80,000Thamel, Boudhanath, Patan metalworking quarter

Singing bowls (also called Himalayan bowls or rin gongs) are hand-hammered bronze or brass bowls that produce a resonant, sustaining tone when struck or rubbed with a wooden mallet. They are used in Tibetan Buddhist meditation, sound healing, and ritual. Antique bowls (pre-1950) are made from a seven-metal alloy (gold, silver, copper, iron, tin, lead, mercury) and produce extraordinarily complex, layered tones. Modern bowls are simpler alloys. The best modern bowls come from the Newari metalworking villages of Patan.

Authenticity tip: Machine-made bowls (imported from India or China) are perfectly uniform in appearance and cheaper. Hand-hammered bowls show hammer marks on the exterior surface under close inspection. Test the tone: a quality bowl sustains its resonance for 20–40 seconds after striking. Antique bowls are increasingly rare and expensive — be very sceptical of "antique" labels on affordable pieces.

4
Khukuri (Gurkha Knife)
NPR 1,500–15,000Thamel, Bhojpur district (for finest quality)

The khukuri (or kukri) is Nepal's national blade — the distinctive forward-curved utility knife and weapon used by Gurkha soldiers for over 200 years. The curved blade, typically 25–38cm in length, is forge-welded from high-carbon steel and traditionally includes two small notches near the handle (the kaura/cho) — one for blood, one for good luck. The handle is made from water buffalo horn, rosewood, or bone. Every Nepali household keeps a khukuri for farming, cooking, and self-defence. Decorative ceremonial khukuris have elaborately decorated sheaths of brass or silver.

Authenticity tip: The finest khukuris come from Bhojpur district in eastern Nepal, where the craft has been practised for generations. Avoid the cheapest tourist shop versions — the steel is poor quality and will not hold an edge. A quality khukuri has a full tang (the metal runs the full length of the handle). Check export regulations — most countries permit khukuri imports but check customs rules for your destination.

5
Dhaka Fabric & Clothing
NPR 800–8,000Tansen (Palpa), Dhankuta, Thamel craft shops

Dhaka is Nepal's most distinctive handwoven fabric — produced on hand looms with intricate geometric patterns in vivid cotton threads. Each producing region (Tansen in western Nepal, Dhankuta and Taplejung in the east) has its own traditional patterns. Products include topis (traditional caps), table runners, bags, scarves, wall hangings, and kurta suits. Dhaka fabric has a characteristic weight and slight stiffness from the supplementary weft threads, and subtle irregularities from the handloom process. It has been granted Geographical Indication (GI) status by Nepal.

Authenticity tip: Machine-made imitations of Dhaka are perfectly uniform and usually lighter in weight. Authentic hand-woven Dhaka has slight variations in the pattern and a heavier, firmer hand. The best place to buy authentic Dhaka is directly in Tansen or from cooperatives that certify fair-trade hand-weaving.

6
Prayer Flags (Lungta & Darchog)
NPR 150–1,500Boudhanath, Thamel, Namche Bazaar

Tibetan Buddhist prayer flags come in two forms: Lungta (horizontal strings of small square flags in the five colours — blue, white, red, green, yellow — representing sky, wind, fire, water, and earth) and Darchog (vertical flags on poles). Printed with the Wind Horse (lungta) carrying wishes to the heavens and surrounded by mantras and sacred symbols, the flags are hung at mountain passes, bridges, home rooftops, and temples. As the wind tears and fades the flags, the prayers are believed to be carried outward into the universe. A set of traditional prayer flags makes one of the most meaningful and lightweight souvenirs from Nepal.

Authenticity tip: Traditional prayer flags are printed with vegetable dyes on cotton or silk and will fade gracefully in the sun. Cheap polyester flags are shiny and hold colour longer — they are not traditional. Woodblock-printed flags from Tibetan refugee workshops around Boudhanath are the most authentic.

7
Lokta Paper Products
NPR 300–3,000Craft shops throughout Kathmandu, Janakpur

Lokta paper is made from the bark of the Daphne plant (Lokta in Nepali), which grows in Nepal's high-altitude forests. It is one of the world's finest handmade papers — strong, slightly textured, insect-resistant, and extraordinarily durable (Nepali historical records on lokta paper survive for 700+ years). Products include notebooks, journals, photo albums, gift wrap, lampshades, cards, and calendars. Some lokta paper is screen-printed with traditional Nepali motifs; some incorporates natural flowers or leaves pressed into the sheet during production. It is a sustainable, entirely natural product.

Authenticity tip: Genuine lokta paper has a slightly irregular, fibrous texture and a warm cream or natural colour before dyeing. It is remarkably strong when torn — try pulling a corner; it tears slowly and shows fibres rather than a clean edge. The Janakpur Women's Development Centre produces beautifully illustrated Mithila-art lokta products.

8
Wooden Masks
NPR 500–15,000Bhaktapur (best quality), Patan, Thamel

Nepal's wooden mask tradition is one of Asia's finest. Masks are used in cham (sacred masked dance) at monasteries and in the festival dances of the Kathmandu Valley. Common subjects include Mahakala (terrifying protector deity), Kumari (the living goddess), Ganesh, Barahi, and various Hindu deities and demons. The finest masks are carved from seasoned wood — typically the wood of a specific sacred tree — by Newar craftspeople in Bhaktapur, painted with natural pigments, and gilded or lacquered. They are both functional ritual objects and outstanding decorative pieces.

Authenticity tip: Factory-made masks from India or China dominate the cheaper end of the market. Nepali hand-carved masks show slight asymmetry and chisel marks in the wood grain. Turn the mask over: genuine Newar carving shows tool marks on the back surface; cast resin imitations are smooth and uniform throughout. Weight is also a guide — real wood is heavier than resin.

9
Bronze / Copper Buddhist & Hindu Statues
NPR 2,000–200,000+Patan (best quality), Boudhanath, Thamel

Nepal's lost-wax (cire perdue) bronze casting tradition has produced some of Asia's finest religious sculpture for over 1,500 years. Modern Newar artisans in Patan continue this tradition, creating statues of Buddhist and Hindu deities in bronze, copper, and brass. Subjects include Buddha, Green Tara, White Tara, Avalokiteshvara, Ganesh, Lakshmi, and Shiva. Quality ranges from mass-produced tourist pieces to museum-quality sculptures that take months to cast and hand-finish. The finishing — cold gilding, stone inlay, and patination — distinguishes fine work from ordinary pieces.

Authenticity tip: Never purchase objects described as "genuine antiques" without export clearance from the Nepal Department of Archaeology — exporting authentic antiques is illegal. Quality modern pieces have crisp detail, even finishing, and correct iconography. The Mahabuddha area of Patan has several renowned studios where you can watch the casting process.

10
Yak Wool Products
NPR 1,500–12,000Namche Bazaar, Thamel, mountain trek routes

Yak wool (from the outer coat) and the finer yak down (from the inner coat, called khullu) are woven into blankets, shawls, hats, socks, sweaters, and bags throughout Nepal's mountain regions. Yak wool is coarser than Pashmina but extremely warm, durable, and water-resistant — ideal for mountain conditions. Naturally occurring colours range from white and grey to brown and black. Blankets and throws are particularly popular; the thick woven texture has a rustic, authentic quality. Chhurpi (yak cheese) in a small quantity is also an edible souvenir, if you dare the rock-hard texture.

Authenticity tip: Pure yak wool has a characteristic slightly coarse, matte texture and earthy natural colours. Acrylic imitations are shinier and lighter. Buy directly from mountain markets (Namche Bazaar, Kyanjin Gompa) or from Sherpa cooperative shops for the most authentic products.

Cultural Keepsakes & Edible Gifts

Souvenirs 11–20

11
Topi (Traditional Nepali Cap)
NPR 300–2,000Thamel, craft markets, Dhaka fabric shops

The Nepali topi is the national headdress for men — a distinctive brimless cap with a slightly tilted asymmetric form, made from Dhaka fabric, velvet, or plain cotton. It is worn on formal occasions, national holidays, and in government settings. Wearing a topi is a patriotic act; Nepal's constitution even specifies that the national dress includes the topi. Available in Dhaka fabric patterns (the most authentic and popular), black velvet (for formal occasions), or embroidered silk. A topi makes a lightweight, uniquely Nepali souvenir that most visitors immediately recognise as Nepali.

Authenticity tip: The best topis are made from authentic Dhaka fabric from Tansen or Dhankuta. Machine-made imitations exist but a well-made Dhaka topi at a fair price (NPR 500–1,200) is easy to find in any good craft shop.

12
Beaded Jewellery (Pote & Tilhari)
NPR 500–8,000Asan market, Indra Chowk, Thamel jewellery shops

Nepali beaded jewellery has a long tradition. The most iconic is the pote — a necklace of tiny glass beads in vivid green and red, worn by married Hindu women as a symbol of their marital status (equivalent to a wedding ring in Western culture). The central pendant is a gold or gold-plated teardrop called tilhari. Other beaded items include prayer beads (mala) in rudraksha seeds, coral, turquoise, and lapis lazuli; Tibetan-style necklaces with metal torque settings; and decorative pieces combining silver and semi-precious stones from the Himalayan region.

Authenticity tip: Turquoise sold in Thamel is frequently dyed howlite or plastic. Genuine turquoise has natural matrix (veins) visible under magnification and is cold to the touch. Rudraksha seeds (the five-faced variety from Nepal) are among the world's finest and significantly more valuable than Indian varieties — verify with a reputable seller.

13
Nepali Tea
NPR 500–4,000 per 100g (premium)Ilam tea estates, specialty tea shops in Kathmandu

Nepal produces exceptional tea — particularly the first and second flush (spring harvest, April–May) teas from the Ilam, Dhankuta, and Taplejung districts in the eastern hills. Nepali tea is closely related to Darjeeling tea (the growing conditions and altitude 1,000–2,000m are similar) and shares its muscatel character and bright, floral quality. Orthodox black tea, green tea, white tea, and oolong are all produced. Brands to look for: Kanchanjangha Tea Estate, Jun Chiyabari (produces excellent single-estate teas), and Ilam Tea. Loose leaf tea packed in traditional tin or cloth packaging makes an excellent, lightweight, and genuinely world-class gift.

Authenticity tip: Buy from specialty tea shops or directly from estate cooperatives rather than generic Thamel tourist shops where blends are common. First flush teas (March–May harvest) command premium prices and have the finest flavour; look for the harvest date on the packaging.

14
Himalayan Coffee
NPR 800–3,500 per 250gSpecialty coffee shops in Thamel and Patan; Gulmi and Lamjung districts

Nepal's coffee industry has grown rapidly, with the Gulmi, Lamjung, Kaski, and Arghakhanchi districts now producing high-quality single-origin arabica at altitudes of 1,000–2,000m. Nepali coffee has a bright acidity, medium body, and floral, nutty character. Roasters such as Himalayan Java, Cafe Mitra, and Moosewala Coffee have put Nepal on the specialty coffee map. Whole bean and ground coffee in sealed bags make excellent, lightweight souvenirs. The environmental and social credentials of Nepal's coffee sector (mostly small-holder organic farmers) are a further selling point.

Authenticity tip: Look for single-origin labelling specifying the district, farm, or cooperative. Blends including Indian robusta are cheaper but lower quality. Most specialty coffee shops in Kathmandu and Pokhara now serve and sell Nepali-origin coffee — taste before you buy.

15
Handmade Notebooks & Journals
NPR 400–2,500Craft shops throughout Kathmandu, Thamel

Nepal produces beautiful handmade notebooks and journals using lokta paper (see #7), often bound in Dhaka fabric, leather, or lokta paper covers with decorative screen-printing. The most charming examples combine lokta paper pages with hand-stamped or woodblock-printed covers depicting Nepali motifs — mandalas, deities, mountain scenes, and ethnic patterns. These make outstanding, practical gifts that are uniquely Nepali and impossible to find elsewhere. Also look for sketchbooks, address books, and photo albums in the same style.

Authenticity tip: The paper quality varies enormously. The best lokta notebooks use thick, well-made paper that won't bleed with most pens. Test by writing on a page with a regular pen before purchasing. Fair trade cooperatives in Kathmandu (such as Mahaguthi and Sana Hastakala) sell consistently high-quality products at fair prices.

16
Paubha Painting
NPR 5,000–300,000+Patan, Bhaktapur, specialist Newar art galleries

Paubha is the Newari equivalent of the Tibetan Thangka — a sacred painting on cloth using mineral and organic pigments with gold, depicting deities and mandalas according to strict iconographic traditions. Unlike Thangkas, Paubhas do not typically have a brocade border and follow the Newar painterly style with slightly different proportions and colour conventions. The tradition has been practised in the Kathmandu Valley for over 1,500 years, and contemporary Newar painters — some trained at the Taragaon Museum and the Siddhi Vinayak arts school — continue producing museum-quality pieces. A fine Paubha is arguably the highest form of traditional Nepali art.

Authenticity tip: As with Thangkas, printed reproductions are sold as originals throughout Thamel. Visit artists' studios in Patan or Bhaktapur and watch the work being done. The finest Paubhas are made with real gold and traditional mineral pigments — costs reflect this. An authentic Paubha of quality is a serious investment and a lifetime heirloom.

17
Stone Carvings
NPR 1,500–50,000Bhaktapur stone carving quarter, Patan, Thamel

Nepali stone carving is one of the world's great sculptural traditions. For visitors, small stone pieces are ideal souvenirs: soapstone incense holders and ashtrays, stone Ganesh figurines, carved OM symbols, Buddha heads, lingam-yoni sets, and architectural replica pieces. The black stone of the Bhaktapur carving tradition (using the local grey-black stone of the valley) is particularly beautiful. Bhaktapur's Pottery Square and the stone-carving workshops along the road from Taumadhi Square are the best places to see craftsmen at work and buy directly.

Authenticity tip: Check for resin cast imitations — these are light, have perfectly uniform texture, and have a slightly shiny surface. Real stone is heavier and cool to the touch. Small carved soapstone pieces are entirely legitimate handmade products; just confirm they are actual stone and not cast resin. Avoid purchasing anything described as an "ancient" artefact — genuine antiques cannot legally be exported.

18
Mani Wheel (Prayer Wheel)
NPR 800–15,000Boudhanath, Thamel, Namche Bazaar

The hand-held prayer wheel (mani chos khor) is one of Tibetan Buddhism's most distinctive objects — a cylindrical drum mounted on a handle, containing a tightly wound scroll of the Om Mani Padme Hum mantra printed thousands of times. Each rotation of the wheel is equivalent to reciting the mantra the number of times it is printed inside. Made in brass, copper, or silver, with embossed mantra text on the exterior. Small, lightweight, and deeply meaningful, a prayer wheel makes one of the finest Buddhist souvenirs from Nepal. Larger fixed prayer wheels are embedded in the walls of stupas and monasteries.

Authenticity tip: Genuine prayer wheels contain a mantra scroll inside — shake gently and you should feel slight weight shifting. The exterior should have Om Mani Padme Hum engraved in Tibetan script. Quality pieces from Boudhanath's Tibetan metalwork shops have noticeably better engraving and smoother rotation than cheap tourist versions.

19
Brass & Copper Water Pots (Gagri & Kalash)
NPR 1,500–20,000Patan metalworking quarter, Bhaktapur, Thamel

Traditional Nepali brass and copper vessels are among the most beautiful everyday objects in South Asian craft traditions. The gagri (tall water pot with a narrow neck and flared lip, carried on the hip) and the kalash (ceremonial water vessel with a mango-leaf and coconut top) are iconic Nepali forms. Both are hammered and shaped from single sheets of metal by Newar craftspeople in Patan's metalworking quarter, then polished to a warm glow. Smaller decorative versions with engraved traditional motifs are sold as ornamental pieces. Copper cookware — dal pots, rice steamers, and thali sets — is also excellent quality.

Authenticity tip: Hand-hammered metal shows concentric hammer marks on the exterior; machine-spun pieces are perfectly smooth. The Patan metalworking quarter (near Mahabuddha) has the highest concentration of quality metalwork workshops in Nepal — buying here supports the living artisanal tradition.

20
Hemp Products
NPR 600–5,000Thamel eco-shops, Pokhara, craft cooperatives

Hemp (bhang in Nepali) grows wild across Nepal's hills and has been used for rope, fabric, and medicine for millennia. Nepal's hemp textile industry is growing, producing bags, backpacks, wallets, clothing, footwear, and accessories from hand-spun and woven hemp fibre. Hemp products are durable, naturally antimicrobial, and environmentally sustainable — hemp requires minimal water and no pesticides. Woven hemp fabric has a pleasantly rough, organic texture that softens with use. Hemp paper (similar to lokta paper in use) is also produced. Products are sold at eco-conscious shops throughout Thamel and Pokhara.

Authenticity tip: Pure hemp fabric is coarser than cotton and has a natural off-white to beige colour. It is very strong — test by pulling a seam. Many "hemp" products are blended with cotton; pure hemp blends are fine but confirm the fibre content with the seller. Fair-trade certified hemp cooperatives are the best source.

Spices, Wellness & Artisan Crafts

Souvenirs 21–30

21
Soapstone Carvings
NPR 500–8,000Bhaktapur, Patan, craft shops throughout Kathmandu

Soapstone (steatite) is a soft, easily carved stone widely used in Nepal for small decorative objects — incense holders, candle stands, decorative plates, deity figures, and architectural detail pieces. It ranges in colour from creamy white to grey, green, and rust. Being soft enough to carve with simple hand tools, soapstone carving is practised widely, but the finest pieces come from Bhaktapur where a long tradition of stone craft endures. The natural smooth, matte surface of well-carved soapstone has an understated elegance. Carved OM symbols, mandalas, and elephant motifs are among the most popular designs.

Authenticity tip: Genuine soapstone is soft enough to be scratched by a fingernail on unpolished surfaces, and is warm and slightly slippery to the touch. Cast resin imitations feel harder and may have slight bubbles or perfectly uniform surface texture. Weight is a reliable guide — real stone is considerably heavier than resin for the same size object.

22
Traditional Pottery
NPR 300–5,000Bhaktapur Pottery Square, Thimi

Bhaktapur's Pottery Square (Kumale Tol) is one of Nepal's most atmospheric artisan quarters — rows of clay pots drying in the sun, potters throwing at kick-wheels in open workshops, and the earthy smell of fired clay. Traditional Nepali pottery is terracotta, unglazed, and fired in outdoor kilns. Products include water pots (gagri), flower pots, oil lamp holders (diyo), incense burners, and decorative plates. The traditional red clay diyo (oil lamp) is a particularly meaningful souvenir — these lamps are lit in every Nepali Hindu home at Tihar. Some contemporary potters also produce glazed decorative ceramics with traditional motifs.

Authenticity tip: Buy directly from Bhaktapur's Pottery Square where you can watch the potter work and buy straight from the maker at the best prices. The experience of watching a master potter throw a perfect pot in minutes is itself unforgettable. Fragile items can be wrapped and packed carefully for travel.

23
Silver Jewellery
NPR 1,500–25,000Indra Chowk, Patan, Thamel silver shops

Nepal has a rich silver jewellery tradition spanning Newar, Tibetan, and contemporary design styles. Traditional pieces include filigree work (delicate wire soldering), repoussé (raised relief designs hammered from behind), stone-set pieces using turquoise, coral, lapis lazuli, and garnet, and cast pieces depicting deities. Contemporary Nepali silversmiths — particularly in Patan's jewellery quarter — produce work that blends traditional iconography with modern aesthetics at competitive prices. Silver content varies — insist on 925 sterling or 999 pure silver and request the hallmark on any significant purchase.

Authenticity tip: The magnet test: genuine silver is not magnetic. The ice test: silver conducts heat very quickly — an ice cube melts noticeably faster on real silver than on steel or alloy. Ask for stamped hallmarks (925 or .999). Reputable shops in Patan will certify silver content. Avoid unmarked pieces sold as "pure silver" by street vendors.

24
Tharu Basket Weaving
NPR 500–4,000Chitwan and Bardia local craft shops, Tharu cultural villages

The Tharu people of Nepal's western Terai are master weavers of both textiles and baskets. Traditional Tharu baskets (dhakiya) are woven from dried grass, bamboo strips, and water reeds in geometric patterns of extraordinary precision. They are used for carrying produce, storing grain, and as ritual objects at weddings and festivals. Small decorative baskets, woven trays, and storage containers with traditional Tharu patterns make beautiful, sustainable souvenirs that directly support the indigenous Tharu communities. Some cooperatives also produce Tharu-style woven textiles in traditional colours and patterns.

Authenticity tip: Purchase directly from Tharu cooperative craft shops near Chitwan or Bardia national parks rather than from Kathmandu gift shops where the provenance is uncertain. Authentic Tharu baskets are woven from natural fibres, not plastic-coated wire — the natural fibres have a matte, organic texture.

25
Spice Mixes (Timur & Nepali Masala)
NPR 200–1,200Asan Market, New Road market, organic spice shops

Nepali cooking uses a distinctive spice palette that includes timur — Nepal's native Sichuan pepper (Zanthoxylum armatum), grown in the mid-hills — which produces a unique numbing, citrusy sensation quite unlike any other spice. Timur is used in momo dipping sauce, chutneys, and meat dishes. Other uniquely Nepali spices include jimbu (Himalayan dried chive/onion grass, used in dal), Himalayan salt, and various custom masala blends for momo, dal, and curry. Pre-packaged spice sets with English labelling are available at organic spice shops in Thamel and Patan. Genuine timur from the hills has an extraordinarily intense aroma.

Authenticity tip: Buy timur from spice markets or organic shops rather than tourist souvenir shops — the quality is significantly better and the price much lower. Genuine timur berries (whole dried) should have an intense citrus-pepper aroma. Ground timur loses its potency quickly — buy whole berries if possible and grind at home.

26
Ayurvedic Products
NPR 300–5,000Ayurvedic pharmacies, Thamel organic shops, Patan

Nepal has a rich Ayurvedic tradition with local herbs — particularly those harvested from the Himalayan forests — that are among the world's finest. Products available include medicinal oils (mahanarayana oil for joint pain, brahmi oil for hair), Chyawanprash (the classic Ayurvedic tonic jam), herbal teas (tulsi, ashwagandha, amalaki), Himalayan herbal soaps, and single herbs (shilajit, ashwagandha, Himalayan ginseng). Dabur and Vaidya Dharmapal are established brands, but smaller artisan producers in Kathmandu often produce superior quality using wild-harvested herbs. The Nepal Ayurveda Association certifies authentic practitioners and products.

Authenticity tip: Shilajit (a resinous mineral substance from Himalayan rock) is widely adulterated — genuine shilajit is a dark, resinous substance that dissolves completely in warm water without sediment. Purchase from certified Ayurvedic pharmacies rather than tourist shops. Check expiry dates on all herbal products.

27
Handmade Candles
NPR 200–2,000Craft shops in Thamel and Patan

Nepal's candle-making tradition combines local beeswax with traditional moulds and fragrance. Candles shaped as stupas, lotus flowers, deity figures, and traditional lamps (diyo) are produced by small workshops, particularly in Patan. Beeswax candles from Nepal's mountain bee-keeping communities have a natural golden colour and honey fragrance. Some producers also use rhododendron honey wax and incorporate dried flowers into transparent candles. The stupa-shaped candle — a replica of Boudhanath in white wax — makes a particularly meaningful souvenir. Fair-trade certified candle cooperatives ensure artisan income.

Authenticity tip: Pure beeswax candles are yellow-golden in colour and have a subtle honey fragrance when unlit. Paraffin candles are white and odourless. Beeswax burns longer and cleaner than paraffin. Some shops blend beeswax and paraffin — ask for the wax content if purity matters to you.

28
Incense & Dhoop (Tibetan Incense)
NPR 150–2,000Boudhanath, Swayambhunath, Thamel, monasteries

Nepal produces some of Asia's finest traditional incense — both the Indian-style thin agarbatti sticks and the distinctly Tibetan thick, round dhoop (incense coils and sticks made by rolling herbal powder around a bamboo core). Tibetan incense uses over 30 medicinal herbs, flowers, resins, and aromatic woods — juniper, rhododendron, spikenard, valerian, myrobalan, and others — ground to powder and rolled by hand into thick, smokeless sticks. The effect is entirely different from synthetic incense: complex, herbal, slightly medicinal, and deeply calming. Monastery-produced incense from Boudhanath's Tibetan communities is the finest.

Authenticity tip: Genuine Tibetan dhoop is thick (5–8mm diameter), dark brown or grey-green in colour, and produces minimal smoke with a complex herbal fragrance. It should smell distinctly different from any commercial Indian incense — more complex and medicinal, less floral. Nado Poizokhang and Tibetan Medical and Astrological Institute brands from Bhutan and Tibet are reference standards.

29
Mandala Art
NPR 1,500–50,000Boudhanath, Thamel art galleries, Patan

The mandala — a geometric representation of the cosmos in Buddhist and Hindu cosmology — is one of Nepal's most distinctive art forms. Mandalas are painted on canvas, paper, or silk using mineral pigments and gold, and can be as small as a postcard or as large as a wall mural. The most complex sand mandalas are created by monks over days or weeks using coloured sand, then ritually destroyed — photographic reproductions of famous sand mandalas are available. For wall art, a well-made painted mandala in traditional mineral pigments is one of Nepal's most beautiful and meaningful souvenirs, combining sacred geometry with extraordinary artistic skill.

Authenticity tip: As with all Nepali art, printed reproductions are common. Hand-painted mandalas show brush strokes under magnification and have slight asymmetries — the geometry is precise but not computer-perfect. Watch for artists working in Boudhanath or Patan and buy from their studios for guaranteed authenticity and the most direct support to the artist.

30
Himalayan Pink Salt
NPR 300–2,000Organic shops in Thamel and Patan, supermarkets

Himalayan rock salt — actually mined primarily from the Khewra mine in Pakistan's Punjab, but sold throughout Nepal due to its geographic proximity — is one of the world's most mineral-rich salts. The distinctive pink colour comes from iron oxide and trace minerals including magnesium, calcium, and potassium. Used as a cooking salt, in salt lamps, in bath soaks, and in Ayurvedic preparations, it is available in coarse and fine grind, as salt slabs for grilling, and as decorative salt lamps. Beautifully packaged Himalayan salt in lokta paper or natural cotton bags makes an excellent edible gift. Nepal also has its own highland salt traditions from the Mustang region.

Authenticity tip: Genuine Himalayan pink salt is mined, not chemically processed — it should have natural variation in colour from pale pink to deep rose. Uniformly deep pink colouring can indicate added dye. Buy from organic or specialty food shops for certified products. Salt lamps are particularly beautiful — when lit, the warm pink glow is genuinely distinctive.
