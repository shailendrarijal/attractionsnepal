Nepal: The World's Only Hindu Kingdom

Historical context of Hinduism as Nepal's state religion

For most of its recorded history, Nepal was the world's only officially Hindu kingdom — a distinction that defined its laws, royal legitimacy, social structure, and cultural expression for centuries. The 2006 People's Movement (Jana Andolan II) ended the 240-year-old Shah monarchy and ushered in a federal democratic republic, and Nepal's 2015 Constitution formally declared the country a secular state. Yet the transformation on paper has not erased what millennia of Hindu civilisation have built: Nepal remains one of the most profoundly Hindu countries on earth in terms of daily practice, cultural expression, and social life.

Hinduism arrived in the Nepal Valley at least 2,000 years ago, brought by Licchavi rulers from the Gangetic plains who established the first great Nepali dynasty (c. 400–750 CE). The Malla kings (c. 1200–1768 CE) were ardent patrons of Hinduism, building the great temples of the Kathmandu Valley, establishing elaborate festival calendars, and integrating the Hindu caste system into Newari society alongside the existing Buddhist traditions. The Shah dynasty, which unified Nepal in 1768 under Prithvi Narayan Shah, maintained Hinduism as the state religion and enshrined it in the Muluki Ain (legal code) of 1854, which codified the caste hierarchy into law.

Today approximately 81% of Nepal's population identifies as Hindu — the highest proportion of any country in the world. But Nepali Hinduism is not monolithic. It incorporates elements of Tantric worship, Tibetan Buddhist influences, shamanic traditions, and ancestor veneration that give it a distinctly Nepali character quite different from the Hinduism of peninsular India. The living goddess Kumari — a pre-pubescent Newar girl worshipped as a manifestation of the divine — is perhaps the clearest symbol of this syncretism.

The Hindu Trinity: Brahma, Vishnu & Shiva

The three supreme manifestations of the divine

At the heart of Hindu theology is the Trimurti — the three aspects of the supreme divine principle (Brahman) that govern the cosmic cycle of creation, preservation, and dissolution:

🌸

Brahma

The Creator. Four-headed, four-armed, holding the Vedas, a water pot, a sceptre, and a lotus. Married to Saraswati (goddess of knowledge). Relatively few temples dedicated to Brahma exist — a myth explains he was cursed by Shiva to not be worshipped. The Swayambhunath area has a small Brahma shrine.

🔱

Vishnu

The Preserver. Blue-skinned, four-armed, holding a conch, discus (chakra), mace, and lotus. Reclines on the cosmic serpent Ananta in the primordial ocean. Has ten principal avatars (dashavatara) including Rama and Krishna. Pashupatinath is technically a form of Vishnu; Changu Narayan is Nepal's finest Vishnu temple.

☯️

Shiva

The Destroyer/Transformer. The most widely worshipped deity in Nepal. Depicted with a third eye, crescent moon in his matted hair, the Ganges flowing from his hair, a trident (trishul), a cobra, and ash-covered body. Worshipped in the form of the linga (phallus/cosmic pillar). Pashupatinath temple is his greatest Nepali shrine.

In Nepal, Shiva is by far the most widely worshipped of the three. The country has more Shiva temples than any other category, and the linga (a stylised column representing Shiva's cosmic energy) is the most common sacred object in Nepali temples. Nepal is sometimes called "Shiva's Country" — a land believed to be under the special protection of the Lord of the Universe.

Major Deities Worshipped in Nepal

The divine family and their manifestations

Pashupatinath — Lord of All Beings

Form of Shiva | Patron deity of Nepal

Pashupatinath ("Lord of Animals / Lord of All Beings") is Nepal's most important deity — the patron god of the entire nation. The Pashupatinath Temple in Kathmandu is his principal shrine and one of the most sacred Shiva temples in the world, drawing pilgrims from across the Hindu world. The main image is a four-faced (chaturthmukha) linga of black stone approximately 1 metre tall, with each face representing a different aspect of Shiva. Worship here is among the most ancient in Nepal, with archaeological evidence suggesting active ritual activity from at least the 5th century CE.

Kumari — The Living Goddess

Pre-pubescent virgin girl, manifestation of Taleju / Durga

The Kumari is a uniquely Nepali institution: a pre-pubescent girl selected from the Shakya (goldsmith) caste of the Newar community through an elaborate ritual process to serve as a living manifestation of the goddess Taleju Bhawani (a form of Durga). She lives in the Kumari Chhen (Kumari House) adjacent to Kathmandu Durbar Square, emerging only for festivals and to bestow blessings on devotees from her window. When she reaches puberty, she is retired with a pension and a new Kumari is selected. The Kathmandu Royal Kumari is the most important, but smaller towns also have their own Kumaris.

Ganesh — Remover of Obstacles

Elephant-headed son of Shiva and Parvati

Ganesh is the most universally beloved deity in Nepal — the elephant-headed god of beginnings, remover of obstacles, and patron of wisdom and learning. His image appears at the entrance of virtually every temple, home, and business in Nepal. No significant undertaking begins without first worshipping Ganesh. He is depicted with one or four arms (holding a broken tusk, a noose, a goad, and a modaka sweet), riding a mouse (representing the ego). The Surya Vinayak and Changunarayan area near Bhaktapur are important Ganesh pilgrimage sites.

Lakshmi — Goddess of Wealth and Prosperity

Consort of Vishnu | Four-armed, seated on lotus

Lakshmi is worshipped throughout Nepal as the bringer of wealth, good fortune, and beauty. During Tihar (Deepawali), homes are thoroughly cleaned and lit with oil lamps to welcome Lakshmi's blessing on the night of Lakshmi Puja (the third day of the festival). She is depicted as a beautiful woman seated or standing on a lotus, with gold coins flowing from her hand. Every shopkeeper's till has a small image of Lakshmi, and the goddess is invoked before any business transaction of significance.

Durga — The Fierce Mother Goddess

Mahishasuramardini — Slayer of the Buffalo Demon

Durga is the supreme manifestation of Shakti (divine feminine power) — the goddess who defeats evil when the male gods cannot. She is depicted riding a lion or tiger, with multiple arms bearing various weapons, in the act of killing the buffalo demon Mahishasura. Dashain, Nepal's greatest festival, celebrates her victory over evil. The Dakshinkali Temple southwest of Kathmandu and the Guhyeshwari Temple near Pashupatinath are important Durga/Shakti shrines. The Taleju Bhawani temples in all three Durbar Squares are also dedicated to a form of Durga.

Hanuman — The Devoted Warrior

The divine monkey general of the Ramayana

Hanuman, the monkey god of devotion and strength, is one of the most popular deities in Nepal. His image — typically depicted as a warrior monkey holding a mountain, mace, or flag — stands guard at palace gates and temple entrances throughout the country. The famous Hanuman Dhoka (Hanuman Gate) in Kathmandu's Durbar Square, where a red-wrapped image of Hanuman has stood since 1672, gave the old royal palace complex its name. Hanuman represents total devotion to God, selfless service, and indomitable courage — qualities that make him a beloved protective figure.

The Four Aims of Human Life

Purushartha — the Hindu framework for a complete life

Hinduism offers a comprehensive framework for human life through the concept of the four Purusharthas (aims of human existence). Understanding these helps explain many aspects of Nepali Hindu social values and daily practice:

1. Dharma — Righteous Duty

Dharma is the principle of cosmic order, right conduct, and moral duty. It encompasses one's obligations as a member of society, a family member, and a being in the cosmos. In Nepal, dharma is inseparable from caste duty (svadharma) — the specific duties appropriate to one's birth, stage of life, and station. It is the foundation upon which the other three aims rest. When Nepalis say "dharmiko kaam" (righteous work), they mean action that maintains harmony with cosmic law.

2. Artha — Wealth and Material Wellbeing

Artha represents the legitimate pursuit of wealth, prosperity, and material security. Hinduism does not reject worldly prosperity — the worship of Lakshmi (goddess of wealth) and Ganesh (remover of obstacles to success) are central religious practices in Nepal. Business success is considered a divine blessing. However, artha must be pursued within the bounds of dharma — unrighteous wealth is forbidden. The Arthashastra of Chanakya is the classic text on this subject.

3. Kama — Love, Pleasure, and Desire

Kama encompasses all forms of desire, love, and sensory pleasure — including but not limited to sexual love. The erotic carvings (kama murals) on the struts of Nepali pagoda temples are expressions of this principle — pleasure as a legitimate aim of human life, integrated into the sacred rather than separated from it. The Kama Sutra of Vatsyayana is the most famous text on kama, but devotional love (bhakti) for a deity is also considered the highest form of kama in the bhakti tradition.

4. Moksha — Liberation from the Cycle of Rebirth

Moksha is the ultimate aim — liberation from the cycle of birth, death, and rebirth (samsara) through the realisation of one's identity with Brahman (ultimate reality). This is the goal of the renunciant (sannyasi), the yogi, and the deeply devout. The sadhus seen at Pashupatinath and other major temples have renounced worldly life entirely in pursuit of moksha. Dying in Pashupatinath's environs or in Varanasi (Kashi) is believed to grant automatic moksha — explaining why elderly and dying Hindus travel there.

Sacred Sites & Hindu Life Rituals

Where Nepali Hindus worship and how they mark life's journey

Five Most Sacred Hindu Sites in Nepal

1

Pashupatinath Temple, Kathmandu (1,337m)

The holiest Hindu site in Nepal and one of the four most sacred Shiva temples in the world (alongside Kashi Vishwanath in Varanasi, Somnath in Gujarat, and Kedarnath in Uttarakhand). The complex spans both banks of the sacred Bagmati River, with the main temple on the west bank inaccessible to non-Hindus but the surrounding complex freely explorable. Active cremation ghats, hundreds of smaller shrines, and an extraordinary concentration of religious life make this one of the most profound sites in all of Asia.

2

Muktinath Temple, Mustang (3,760m)

One of the 108 Divya Desam (sacred Vishnu temples) and considered one of the Mukti Kshetras (places of liberation). The natural eternal flames (gas jets from the rock) and sacred springs are considered miraculous manifestations of all five elements simultaneously — earth, water, fire, air, and sky. Pilgrims undergo ritual bathing under the 108 water spouts to achieve purification. Located in the dramatic high-altitude Mustang landscape, the journey here is itself considered a meritorious pilgrimage.

3

Manakamana Temple, Gorkha (1,302m)

The "wish-fulfilling" goddess Bhagwati grants the desires of sincere devotees according to centuries of tradition. One of Nepal's most visited pilgrimage sites, accessible by cable car from the Trisuli valley. The temple complex sits on a high ridge above the confluence of the Trisuli and Marsyangdi rivers, with sweeping views of the Ganesh Himal range. Animal sacrifice is performed here as part of votive offerings, particularly of goats and chickens on festival days.

4

Devghat, Tanahu (Chitwan/Tanahu border, 300m)

The confluence (triveni) of the Trishuli and Kaligandaki rivers, considered a sacred tirtha (crossing place between human and divine worlds). Devghat is one of Nepal's most important pilgrimage sites, particularly for elderly Hindus who come here to spend their final days and achieve moksha by dying at a tirtha. The hermit colony of sadhus and sages who live in ashrams along the river is one of the most authentic such communities in Nepal.

5

Gosaikunda Lake, Langtang (4,380m)

According to Hindu mythology, Shiva struck his trident into the Himalayan rock to create this lake's spring after drinking the ocean's poison (halahala) to save the universe during the churning of the cosmic ocean. The turquoise lake at 4,380m is a powerful place of pilgrimage, especially during Janai Purnima (July–August full moon) when hundreds of thousands of pilgrims trek here to bathe and change their sacred thread (janai). Lord Shiva is believed to reside in the lake during the summer months.

Temple Etiquette for Visitors

How to visit Hindu temples respectfully and without causing offence

Always Do

  • Remove shoes before entering the temple complex (shoe racks are provided; NPR 10–20 fee common)
  • Dress modestly — shoulders and knees covered; scarves available for hire at major temples
  • Walk clockwise (pradakshina) around the main shrine
  • Keep voices low and behaviour respectful
  • Accept prasad (blessed food offered by the priest) with both hands or right hand
  • Ask permission before photographing rituals or worshippers
  • Make a small donation in the donation box if you have enjoyed the experience
  • Observe and follow the lead of worshippers for rituals you participate in
  • Keep the temple premises clean — do not bring outside food or drink

Never Do

  • Enter the inner sanctum of a Hindu temple if you are not Hindu — many are strictly closed to non-Hindus (Pashupatinath main temple, Taleju Bhawani in Kathmandu)
  • Wear leather inside a temple (belt, shoes, bags) — leather from cows is particularly prohibited in Shiva and Vishnu temples
  • Touch the sacred images or linga unless specifically invited by a priest
  • Enter while menstruating (traditional restriction still observed at many temples)
  • Enter if you have recently been in contact with a corpse or have not bathed after a funeral
  • Photograph the main deity image without permission — some temples prohibit this entirely
  • Wear shoes or sandals — remove them completely at the entrance
  • Show disrespect to the cremation ghats at Pashupatinath — maintain solemn distance
  • Accept food or prasad with your left hand alone

Understanding the Tika and Prasad

When a Hindu priest places a tika (red vermillion paste, sometimes mixed with rice grains) on your forehead, this is a blessing — accept it graciously with a slight bow. The tika applied at Dashain by elders is the most sacred: a mixture of red powder, curd, and rice that represents the goddess Durga's blessing. Prasad — blessed food that has been offered to the deity — is distributed to worshippers after puja. Common prasads include flower petals, fruits, sweets (laddu, pedha), and in some temples cooked rice. Always accept prasad with respect; it is considered the deity's grace made edible.
