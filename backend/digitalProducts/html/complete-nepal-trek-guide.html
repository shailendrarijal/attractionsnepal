Introduction to Trekking in Nepal

Everything you need to know before you lace up your boots

Nepal is home to eight of the world's fourteen peaks above 8,000 metres, and the trekking routes that wind through its valleys, ridges, and high passes represent some of the most rewarding wilderness journeys on earth. Whether you dream of standing at Everest Base Camp at 5,364m, circling the Annapurna massif through ancient trading villages, or walking quietly through the rhododendron forests of the Langtang Valley, Nepal delivers an unmatched combination of natural grandeur, cultural richness, and raw adventure.

Trekking in Nepal is accessible to anyone in reasonable health. Routes range from gentle valley walks completed in four or five days to multi-week expeditions crossing remote high-altitude passes that demand prior mountain experience. The country's network of tea-houses (locally called bhatti or lodge) means you can travel with a light pack, sleeping and eating at family-run guesthouses every evening — a uniquely Nepali form of trekking that lets you engage with local communities throughout your journey.

Why Trek in Nepal?

  • Scale: No other country on earth concentrates so many high peaks within such a small area. On a single trek you may pass beneath five or six distinct peaks above 7,000m.
  • Culture: Trekking routes pass through living villages of Sherpa, Tamang, Gurung, Magar, and Thakali communities, each with distinct languages, temples, and traditions.
  • Infrastructure: The tea-house network on popular routes is well-developed. Hot meals, warm beds, and phone charging are available even at altitudes above 5,000m on the EBC trail.
  • Value: Nepal remains one of the most affordable adventure destinations in the world. A full Everest Base Camp trek including flights, permits, accommodation, and food can cost as little as USD 1,200 if done independently.
  • Wilderness: Remote routes such as Kanchenjunga and the Dolpo region offer genuine wilderness experiences with few other trekkers and minimal infrastructure.

Essential Permits for Nepal Trekking

All foreign trekkers require permits to enter Nepal's trekking regions. The exact permits depend on where you trek.

TIMS Card

Trekkers' Information Management System. Required for most routes. USD 20 (individual) or USD 10 (group). Obtain in Kathmandu at the Nepal Tourism Board or Pokhara.

ACAP Permit

Annapurna Conservation Area Project. USD 30 per person. Required for all Annapurna region treks including ABC, Annapurna Circuit, Poon Hill.

SNPP / Sagarmatha NP

Sagarmatha National Park entry for Everest region. NPR 3,000 (approx. USD 22). Paid at Monjo checkpoint on the trail.

Restricted Area Permits

Manaslu, Upper Mustang, Dolpo, Kanchenjunga, and other restricted zones require special permits costing USD 70–500 depending on route and season. Must be obtained with a licensed guide.

Langtang NP

Langtang National Park entry permit. NPR 3,000. Covers Langtang Valley, Gosaikunda, and Helambu routes.

Gaurishankar CAP

Gaurishankar Conservation Area Permit. NPR 3,000. Required for Rolwaling Valley treks near the Tibetan border.

Tip: Get Permits in Kathmandu or Pokhara

The Nepal Tourism Board office in Kathmandu (Pradarshani Marg, next to BICC) is the most convenient one-stop location for TIMS cards and most conservation area permits. Bring 2 passport photos and a photocopy of your passport. Some permits can now be obtained online at online.ntb.gov.np before you arrive.

Do I Need a Guide or Porter?

Since April 2023, the Government of Nepal requires all foreign trekkers to be accompanied by a licensed guide on most popular routes, including EBC, Annapurna Circuit, Manaslu, and others. This rule was introduced partly for safety reasons following a number of solo trekker deaths. You may still trek independently on some lower-altitude routes, but check current regulations with the Nepal Tourism Board before departure. Even where not legally required, hiring a local guide enriches the experience enormously and provides vital support at altitude. Porters typically carry up to 25kg and are paid approximately NPR 700–1,000 per day plus food and accommodation.

Top 10 Trek Routes in Nepal

From classic classics to remote wilderness — routes for every ambition

1. Everest Base Camp Trek (EBC)

14 days Hard Max: 5,364m Most Popular

Starting point: Lukla (fly from Kathmandu, 35 min) | Region: Khumbu, Solukhumbu District

The Everest Base Camp Trek is the most famous trekking route in the world, and rightly so. From the moment you land at Tenzing-Hillary Airport in Lukla — itself an adventure — you follow the Dudh Koshi River through rhododendron and pine forests, past Sherpa villages, Buddhist monasteries, and enormous mani stone walls. The trail passes through Namche Bazaar (3,440m), the bustling trading hub of the Khumbu, before climbing to Tengboche Monastery (3,860m), whose setting beneath Ama Dablam is one of the most photographed views in Nepal.

The final approach to Base Camp traverses the boulder-strewn Khumbu Glacier moraine, with the Khumbu Icefall visible above. Even in the trekking season, Base Camp is a city of tents belonging to climbing expeditions. The Kala Patthar viewpoint (5,545m) above Gorak Shep offers the best accessible view of Everest's summit pyramid.

Highlights: Namche Bazaar, Tengboche Monastery, Ama Dablam views, Khumbu Glacier, Kala Patthar sunrise, Sherpa culture, Sagarmatha National Park wildlife (Himalayan tahr, snow leopard tracks).

2. Annapurna Circuit Trek

15–20 days Moderate–Hard Max: 5,416m (Thorong La)

Starting point: Besisahar (bus from Kathmandu or Pokhara) | Region: Annapurna, Manang, Mustang

Widely regarded as one of the greatest long-distance treks in the world, the Annapurna Circuit encircles the entire Annapurna Massif, crossing the Thorong La Pass at 5,416m. The route transitions through an extraordinary range of landscapes and climate zones: subtropical lowlands give way to temperate forests, then alpine meadows, and finally the arid Tibetan-influenced plateau of the Mustang region north of the main Himalayan range. The cultural diversity is equally striking — you walk through Gurung, Manangi, and Thakali villages, each with distinct customs and architecture.

Highlights: Thorong La Pass crossing, Muktinath Temple, Kagbeni medieval village, Pisang monastery, Manang acclimatisation day, Marpha apple orchards, Tatopani hot springs, Poon Hill sunrise (optional detour).

3. Annapurna Base Camp Trek (ABC)

7–12 days Moderate Max: 4,130m

Starting point: Nayapul or Phedi (from Pokhara) | Region: Annapurna Sanctuary

The Annapurna Base Camp trek leads into the heart of the Annapurna Sanctuary, a high glacial amphitheatre completely enclosed by a ring of peaks exceeding 6,000–8,000m. Annapurna I (8,091m), Machhapuchhre (6,993m — the fish-tail peak), Hiunchuli, and Gangapurna all loom overhead. The approach through Modi Khola gorge is spectacular, with rhododendron forests, Gurung villages, and hot springs at Jhinu Danda along the way. ABC makes a superb introduction to Himalayan trekking without requiring the endurance of the full Circuit.

Highlights: Annapurna Sanctuary amphitheatre, Machhapuchhre Base Camp, Ghorepani and Poon Hill sunrise (usually included), Gurung villages of Ghandruk and Chhomrong, Modi Khola gorge, rhododendron forests in bloom (March–April).

4. Langtang Valley Trek

7–10 days Moderate Max: 4,984m (Tsergo Ri optional)

Starting point: Syabrubesi (bus from Kathmandu, 7–8 hours) | Region: Langtang National Park

The Langtang Valley is often called "the valley of glaciers," and it earns that name. Located just 50km north of Kathmandu, the valley was devastated by the 2015 earthquake and subsequent landslide that buried the village of Langtang, but it has rebuilt with extraordinary resilience. The rebuilt villages are welcoming, and the scenery — with Langtang Lirung (7,227m), Dorje Lakpa, and Naya Kanga framing the valley — is breathtaking. The trail passes through dense forest full of red pandas, then enters high alpine terrain with yak pastures and traditional Tamang and Tibetan-influenced communities.

Highlights: Kyanjin Gompa monastery, Kyanjin Ri viewpoint (4,773m), Tsergo Ri (4,984m), yak cheese factory at Kyanjin, traditional Tamang villages, red panda habitat, views of Langtang Lirung and Dorje Lakpa.

5. Manaslu Circuit Trek

14–16 days Hard Max: 5,160m (Larkya La) Restricted Area

Starting point: Soti Khola (bus from Kathmandu) | Region: Gorkha, Manaslu Conservation Area

The Manaslu Circuit is widely considered the last great circuit trek of Nepal — equally spectacular to the Annapurna Circuit but with far fewer trekkers. The route circles the world's eighth-highest mountain, Manaslu (8,163m), crossing the Larkya La pass at 5,160m. The trail follows the Budhi Gandaki River through dramatic gorges, past remote Tibetan Buddhist villages, and over high alpine terrain before descending to the Annapurna region. Because it's a restricted area, trekkers must travel with a licensed guide and obtain a special permit.

Highlights: Larkya La pass crossing, views of Manaslu summit, Tibetan Buddhist gompa at Samagaon, Birendra Lake, Pungen Glacier, Tsum Valley extension (optional), genuine remoteness and authentic culture.

6. Upper Mustang Trek

10–12 days Moderate Max: 3,840m (Lo Manthang) Restricted Area

Starting point: Jomsom (fly from Pokhara) | Region: Mustang, former Kingdom of Lo

Upper Mustang is the former forbidden Kingdom of Lo, which only opened to trekkers in 1992. It remains one of the most culturally intact Tibetan Buddhist kingdoms in the world. The landscape is unlike anything else in Nepal — stark, eroded cliffs of ochre and crimson, ancient cave monasteries carved into cliff faces, and the medieval walled capital of Lo Manthang with its palace, monasteries, and traditional architecture. Because it lies north of the main Himalayan range, Upper Mustang is in a rain shadow and trekking is excellent even during the monsoon (June–September).

Highlights: Lo Manthang walled city, Thubchen Gompa 15th-century frescoes, Chhoser cave monastery, Ghar Gompa, ancient sky caves, Chosar and Dhee villages, extraordinary eroded landscape, yak caravans, Tibetan Buddhist festivals.

7. Kanchenjunga Trek

18–20 days Extreme Max: 5,143m (North Base Camp) Restricted Area

Starting point: Taplejung (fly from Kathmandu) | Region: Far Eastern Nepal, Taplejung District

The Kanchenjunga trek is one of the most remote and demanding treks in Nepal, reaching the base camps of the world's third-highest mountain (8,586m). Most itineraries visit both the North Base Camp (Pangpema, 5,143m) and South Base Camp (Oktang, 4,730m), requiring a long and strenuous traverse. The reward is complete wilderness: few other trekkers, pristine forests, blooming rhododendrons of extraordinary size, and unobstructed views of the Kanchenjunga massif's enormous east face. The trail passes through Rai and Limbu communities of the eastern hills before entering high alpine terrain.

Highlights: Pangpema North Base Camp panorama, Oktang South Base Camp glacier views, Ghunsa Tibetan village, Lhonak valley, Kambachen glaciers, pristine rhododendron and oak forests, Rai and Limbu culture, genuine remoteness.

8. Rara Lake Trek

10–12 days Moderate Max: 3,962m (Rara Lake)

Starting point: Jumla (fly from Nepalgunj) | Region: Mugu District, Mid-Western Nepal

Rara Lake, at 3,962m altitude and covering 10.8 square kilometres, is Nepal's largest lake — a jewel of cerulean blue set in a bowl of pine and juniper forest in one of the least-visited corners of the country. The trek from Jumla passes through Kalikot and Mugu districts, remote hill villages where electricity and mobile phone coverage are still rare. The lake's colour changes throughout the day from deep blue to aquamarine, and in clear weather the surrounding peaks reflect perfectly on its surface. Wildlife around Rara includes Himalayan black bear, red panda, musk deer, and numerous bird species.

Highlights: Rara Lake itself — Nepal's largest and most beautiful lake, ancient Sinja Valley (former medieval capital), Chere Chaur meadows, pristine conifer forests, extraordinary birdlife, genuine off-the-beaten-path experience, Mugu people's traditional lifestyle.

9. Khopra Danda Ridge Trek

8–10 days Moderate Max: 4,900m (Khopra Ridge)

Starting point: Nayapul (from Pokhara) | Region: Annapurna Conservation Area, Myagdi

Khopra Danda (also called Khopra Ridge) is a superb alternative to the Poon Hill trek that sees far fewer trekkers while offering equally spectacular — and in many ways superior — views. The trail climbs through rhododendron forests to the high ridge of Khopra Danda, where a community-run lodge sits at nearly 3,700m with unobstructed views of Dhaulagiri (8,167m), Annapurna South, Annapurna I, Nilgiri, and Tukuche Peak. An extension to Khair Lake (4,500m), a sacred Hindu lake set among high alpine terrain, makes for an excellent day hike from the ridge.

Highlights: Khopra Ridge 360° mountain panorama, Khair Lake sacred site, Swanta village traditional architecture, superb Dhaulagiri and Annapurna views, rhododendron forests in bloom, far fewer crowds than Poon Hill, excellent local community lodges.

10. Gosaikunda Trek

4–5 days Moderate Max: 4,380m (Gosaikunda Lake)

Starting point: Dhunche or Sundarijal (bus from Kathmandu) | Region: Langtang National Park

Gosaikunda is one of Nepal's most sacred Hindu and Buddhist lakes, set at 4,380m in the Langtang National Park. According to Hindu mythology, Lord Shiva struck his trident into the Himalayan rock to create the lake's spring after drinking poison to save the universe. During the full moon of Shravan (July–August), hundreds of thousands of Hindu pilgrims make the journey to Gosaikunda for the Janai Purnima festival. The trek from Dhunche passes through magnificent forest and high ridges before reaching the sacred lake, and can be extended over the Laurebina Pass (4,610m) to connect with the Helambu circuit.

Highlights: Sacred Gosaikunda Lake and Hindu mythology, Laurebina Yak viewpoint, Helambu extension via Laurebina Pass, Tamang and Sherpa villages, rhododendron forests, Langtang National Park wildlife, Ganesh Himal and Langtang range views.

Altitude Sickness: Prevention & Treatment

The most critical safety knowledge for every high-altitude trekker

Acute Mountain Sickness (AMS) is the most common medical emergency on Nepal's trekking routes, and it is entirely preventable with proper acclimatisation. Every year, trekkers are evacuated — and some die — because they push too high too fast. Understanding altitude illness is not optional; it is fundamental knowledge for anyone venturing above 3,000m.

What is Altitude Sickness?

As you ascend, atmospheric pressure decreases and the partial pressure of oxygen drops. At 5,000m, each breath delivers roughly half the oxygen available at sea level. Your body responds by breathing faster, producing more red blood cells, and increasing heart rate. When ascent is too rapid, this physiological adjustment cannot keep pace and altitude illness results. There are three distinct syndromes:

AMS – Acute Mountain Sickness

Onset: Typically 6–12 hours after arriving at new altitude
Symptoms: Headache (the cardinal symptom), nausea, fatigue, dizziness, loss of appetite, poor sleep, mild shortness of breath at rest
Treatment: Stop ascending. Rest at current altitude. Drink 3–4 litres of water per day. Take Ibuprofen or Paracetamol for headache. Take Diamox 125–250mg twice daily. If symptoms do not improve within 24 hours, descend at least 500m immediately.

HACE – High Altitude Cerebral Edema (Life-threatening)

Onset: Usually 1–2 days after severe or unresolved AMS
Symptoms: Severe persistent headache, loss of coordination (ataxia — test by walking heel-to-toe in a straight line), confusion, drowsiness, inability to walk, loss of consciousness
Treatment: THIS IS A MEDICAL EMERGENCY. Descend immediately — even 300–500m can be lifesaving. Give Dexamethasone 8mg immediately, then 4mg every 6 hours. Use a Gamow bag if available. Evacuate to lower altitude as fast as possible by any means.

HAPE – High Altitude Pulmonary Edema (Life-threatening)

Onset: Usually 2–4 days after rapid ascent above 2,500m
Symptoms: Extreme fatigue, dry cough progressing to frothy or pink sputum, severe breathlessness at rest, cyanosis (blue lips/fingertips), crackling sounds when breathing
Treatment: THIS IS A MEDICAL EMERGENCY. Descend immediately — minimum 1,000m. Give Nifedipine 30mg extended release (or 10mg immediate release, repeat in 1 hour). Administer supplemental oxygen if available. Evacuate to hospital immediately. HAPE kills faster than HACE.

The Golden Rules of Acclimatisation

  1. Climb high, sleep low: Day hikes to higher altitude before sleeping lower are the best acclimatisation strategy. On the EBC trail, the Namche–Everest View Hotel day hike exemplifies this perfectly.
  2. Never ascend more than 300–500m of sleeping altitude per day above 3,000m: From Namche (3,440m), your next sleeping altitude should not exceed 3,800–3,900m.
  3. Take rest days: Plan at least one acclimatisation rest day for every 1,000m gained above 3,000m. Namche Bazaar and Dingboche are the standard rest-day stops on EBC.
  4. Stay hydrated: Drink 3–4 litres of water daily. Dehydration dramatically worsens AMS symptoms.
  5. Avoid alcohol and sedatives at altitude: Both suppress respiration and worsen acclimatisation.
  6. Go slowly: "Pole, pole" (Swahili for "slowly, slowly") applies equally in Nepal. Your pace should allow normal conversation.
  7. Never ignore symptoms: AMS headache + vomiting = stop. AMS + any ataxia or confusion = HACE. DESCEND NOW.
  8. Trust the lake-test: Spit-test for ataxia — can you walk heel-to-toe along a straight line? If not, descend immediately.

Diamox (Acetazolamide) — Should You Take It?

Diamox is a carbonic anhydrase inhibitor that stimulates deeper breathing and accelerates acclimatisation. A standard preventive dose is 125mg (half a tablet) twice daily, starting 24 hours before ascending above 2,500m. Side effects include tingling in fingers and toes (very common, harmless), increased urination, and occasionally blurred vision. Diamox does NOT mask symptoms of serious altitude illness — it genuinely accelerates acclimatisation. Do NOT use if allergic to sulphonamide drugs. Consult a travel medicine doctor before your trek.

Best Teahouses & Sample EBC Itinerary

Where to stay and a day-by-day plan for the world's most iconic trek

Understanding the Tea-House System

Nepal's tea-house network is one of the wonders of adventure travel. Family-run guesthouses dot the main trekking routes, offering a simple bed, hot meals, and the warmth of Sherpa, Tamang, and Gurung hospitality. Rooms cost NPR 200–500 (USD 1.50–4) per person on most routes, with the convention being that accommodation is offered cheaply in exchange for eating all meals at the lodge. Meals cost NPR 400–800 (USD 3–6) each. Budget approximately USD 20–30 per day for accommodation, meals, and incidental expenses. The following teahouses consistently receive high praise:

Sample 14-Day EBC Day-by-Day Itinerary

D1

Kathmandu → Lukla (fly) → Phakding (2,610m)

Early morning 35-minute flight to Tenzing-Hillary Airport, Lukla. Trek downhill along the Dudh Koshi River to Phakding. Easy 3-hour walk — settle in, acclimatise gently. Distance: 8km.

D2

Phakding → Namche Bazaar (3,440m)

Cross several suspension bridges over the Dudh Koshi gorge. The final 600m climb to Namche is steep and demanding. First views of Everest summit above the Lhotse-Nuptse wall. Distance: 11km, +700m ascent.

D3

Namche Bazaar — Acclimatisation Day

Rest day in Namche. Morning hike to Everest View Hotel (3,880m) for your first full view of Everest. Afternoon: explore Sherpa Museum, Namche market (Saturday is market day). Excellent bakeries and coffee shops.

D4

Namche → Tengboche (3,860m)

Beautiful walk through rhododendron and juniper forest on the Khumbu valley high route. Tengboche Monastery — the largest in the Khumbu — sits in an extraordinary position beneath Ama Dablam. Distance: 10km.

D5

Tengboche → Dingboche (4,410m)

Descend to Pangboche, visit the oldest monastery in the Khumbu (Pangboche Gompa), then climb through open valley to Dingboche with classic Ama Dablam views. Distance: 10km, +550m.

D6

Dingboche — Acclimatisation Day

Day hike to Nagarjun Hill (5,100m) for views of Makalu, Lhotse, Nuptse, and Cho Oyu. Or hike to Chhukhung Ri (5,550m) for ambitious acclimatisers. Return to Dingboche to sleep.

D7

Dingboche → Lobuche (4,940m)

Cross the Khumbu Glacier lateral moraine. Pass the Thukla memorial cairns honouring climbers lost on Everest. Lobuche is cold and barren — a true high-altitude outpost. Distance: 8km, +530m.

D8

Lobuche → Gorak Shep (5,164m) → Everest Base Camp (5,364m)

Trek to Gorak Shep (last teahouse), drop packs, then make the 3-hour return hike across the Khumbu moraine to Base Camp. An unforgettable experience. Return to Gorak Shep for the night. Total: 14km.

D9

Gorak Shep → Kala Patthar (5,545m) → Pheriche (4,240m)

Pre-dawn start for Kala Patthar summit — the best viewpoint of Everest accessible without technical climbing. Sunrise over the South Col is magnificent. Descend all the way to Pheriche for better sleep altitude. Distance: 16km.

D10

Pheriche → Namche Bazaar (3,440m)

Long descent day following the Imja Khola and Dudh Koshi valleys. Knees will feel the 1,200m descent. Namche feels like a luxury town after high camps. Distance: 20km.

D11

Namche Bazaar → Lukla (2,860m)

Final long descent day on familiar trail. Celebrate at the many teahouses and cafés in Lukla with fellow trekkers. Optional: Hot shower, pizza, apple pie at the famous Café de Mountain. Distance: 19km.

D12

Lukla → Kathmandu (fly) — Buffer Day

Flights from Lukla are weather-dependent and frequently delayed or cancelled. Always build 1–2 buffer days into your itinerary. If flight is cancelled, spend time exploring Lukla market.

D13–14

Buffer / Kathmandu Celebration

Kathmandu: post-trek celebration dinner in Thamel, shopping for souvenirs and certificates, visit Garden of Dreams or Boudhanath Stupa. Many trekkers treat themselves to a Thamel restaurant for their first real meal off the mountain.

Important: Always Buy Helicopter Evacuation Insurance

Nepal's mountain rescue relies on helicopters. A single rescue flight from Everest Base Camp to Kathmandu costs USD 4,000–8,000. Comprehensive trekking insurance with helicopter evacuation coverage is mandatory — not optional. Companies like World Nomads, True Traveller, and Battleface offer suitable policies. Carry your insurance details and emergency contact numbers on the trail at all times.
